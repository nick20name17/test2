How It's Made

Old Ways, Every Time

01

The Mash

Non-GMO yellow dent corn, malted barley, and mountain spring water. Cooked low and slow for a full 24 hours.

02

Open-air fermentation in 50-gallon cypress barrels. 7–10 days. No shortcuts, no accelerators.

The Still

Single-run through Grandpa's original copper pot still. We only take the heart cut. Everything else goes back in.

Every jar is filled, sealed, and labeled by hand. Batch number and date written with a Sharpie. Just like always.

Our Story

Three generations. One copper pot. Zero compromises.

Where It All Started

In the summer of 1962, a 24-year-old man named Earl Tibbs built a copper pot still in the shed behind his house in the Blue Ridge Mountains of North Carolina. He'd learned to distill from his own grandfather, who'd learned from his. The recipe was passed down not as a document, but as a conversation — measured in handfuls, counted by smell, guided by feel.

Grandpa Earl at his still, 1968. That's the same copper pot we use today.

The Craft

Earl never cut corners. His philosophy was simple: use the best grain you can find, use clean mountain water, ferment slow, and only take the heart cut from the still. The heads and tails — the parts that would make your head pound the next morning — went straight back into the next batch. "If it ain't the good part," he'd say, "it ain't going in the jar."

Three Generations On

Today, Earl's grandchildren run the operation. The still has been restored, the shed has been expanded into a proper distillery, and we have a license now — something Grandpa would've found very amusing. But the corn mash recipe hasn't changed, the fermentation time hasn't changed, and the copper pot is the same one Earl built in that shed in 1962. Some things, you just don't fix.
